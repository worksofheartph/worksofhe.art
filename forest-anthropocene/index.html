VISIT SITE

The Philippines has gradually and significantly lost its tropical forest over time with deforestation and forest degradation, the biggest evidence of its destruction. The Anthropocene or the Age of Man is the proposed new geological age wherein it focuses on the human and nature dynamics that impact our climate and environment. Forest Foundation Philippines has compiled case studies and professionals’ experiences in the field to understand how to change and implement more conservation projects in this new epoch.

TELLING THE STORIES OF OUR FORESTS

Forest Foundation Philippines is a non-profit organization who provides opportunities to other organizations who share the same vision of protecting the forests. They assembled a multidisciplinary research team to create Forests in the Anthropocene: Perspectives from the Philippines. As a part of the Foundation’s Learning Landscapes Program, this book that aims to provide local accounts of human and forest dynamics, and probe into the historical trajectories and conservation experiences within the mosaics of forest landscapes across the archipelago.

How can we continuously implement conservation projects? How can we re-establish a better interaction with our environment? We need to go back a page, take a step back and understand what we lacked? where we did good, and where we can do better. Each chapter in Forests in the Anthropocene focuses on each interdisciplinary perspectives in this epoch. Each delves deeper into understanding social experiences, ushering sustainability issues, perspectives on action that can be taken in governance, and various contributions of impact to the forests.

Chapter One introduces us with the overview of human and nature dynamics with social, political, economic, and cultural influences taking part in the forests degradation. It leads us to the understanding of how these challenges are becoming more apparent and how we can put the human nature touch we hold for the environment. Chapter Two then places analyses of past and existing conservation projects. They show how social network analysis (SNA) can be used as a project evaluation tool for community-based forestry projects. It applies this approach with the tool to two projects which engage with six community-based forestry initiatives in the Mt. Kalatungan Mountain Range. This also opens up opportunities in collaboration for sustainability in communities surround the remaining forests.

As a big part of the implementation and protection of the forests, Chapter Three highlights the experiences and discussions from our forest guards around Luzon and Northern Mindanao. The little that is chronicled of the achievements, studies, livelihood, and overall role of these forest guards contributes to the challenges and motivation of protecting the forests we still have. Opportunities on scholarships and the political aspects of this sector shows us the relevance of their continued work. Similar with Chapter Three, Chapter Four delves deeper into contributions of the livelihood in communities surrounding the remaining lush of forest. The case study focuses on the research assessment in the Higaonon community where the use of giant honey bees and abacá plants shape livelihoods of indigenous peoples and the ecological integrity of the forest landscape. Chapters Five and Six are studies that concentrates on other factors in the environment, mainly water and energy sector, that also contribute to the destructions in the Age of Man. Both chapters highlight the decision-making and policy-making for each sector and how it creates impact to the over all challenges we are facing now.

DESIGNING BY THE TRACES OF ITS RINGS

It was important to balance the urgency and seriousness of the topic with a sense of hope—once we are equipped with the lessons of our past and the trends of our present, we have the capacity to change the future of our forests. To visually represent this message, we anchored the creative direction of the book and website on tree rings, which, besides showing us a picture of our history, also give us an idea of our future. When the book is held, the reader's thumb aligns with the center of the tree rings on the cover, emphasizing humankind's imprint and influence on the natural world.

It's about the people in the environment; it's about the environment for the people.
Images and visual cues taken from the trip to the Yangil Nursery informed our creative direction and inspired the formation of our logo and assets.

Each chapter opens with an illustrated abstract which feature a variety of actors—indigenous peoples, scientists, researchers, forest guards—emphasizing the collective work required to protect our forests. In-line illustrations support the narrative of every chapter and help ground these scholarly articles in reality. The textures from the illustrations and the technical grid of the layout represent the blend of indigenous and scientific knowledge necessary to effectively manage our forests.

  
EPILOGUE AND PROLOGUE

This project aims to place this accessible lens into the future we can make wherein anyone can support and implement the conservation of our tropical  forests as well as  keeping our natural resources, community livelihood, and socio-economical solutions abundant.

In this age of man, how can we protect our forests? How can humankind be kind to nature?

We encourage policymakers, researchers, and students to promote more breathable forests and abundance. All of us have different roles in contributing to our forests’ disruption thus, we all have our own roles in saving it. Forests in the Anthropocene, in its current form as a book or publication, is finite—it has a last page— but everyone who worked in this project has an extended role to spread the content within its pages and reach those people who we need, who our forests need the most.
